Basic modeling assumptions

  • Linearity
    • Linear relationship between outcome and predictors (constant change)
  • Normality
    • Errors (not variables!) ‚Üí Normally distributed (least important)
  • Equal variance (Homoskedasticity)

    • Errors (residuals) ‚Üí Independent (i.e., uncorrelated): Fundamental

  • Loss of information:
    • Reduction of statistical power (i.e., ability to detect an effect if it truly exists)
  • p-values ‚Üí Artificially low ‚Üì

  • In short:
    • Our inferences can be seriously compromised (less accurate)

    • Innovative method ‚Üí Mixed-effects modeling

Mixed-effects models

What they are

  • Extension of traditional regression
    • But can incorporate all kinds of dependencies into the analysis
  • Examples of nesting and dependencies:
    • Students within classes (within schools)
    • Observations within people (e.g., longitudinal data)
    • Observations within pairs
    • Patients within hospitals
    • People within cities
    • And so on‚Ä¶

Mixed-effects model

Outcome = Intercept + Slope * predictor + Error

Mixed-effects models

Advantages over traditional methods

  • Allow researchers to avoid averaging over the data
  • More flexible ‚Üí Can deal with other types of outcome data (e.g., binary)
  • Handle missing data and unbalanced designs very well
  • Allow estimation:
    • Multiple effects on outcome variable (e.g., the effect of age and
      feedback on language scores), and
    • Individual variation within and across subjects and items
  • Put simply, they allow simultaneous modeling of fixed and random effects
    • Hence, the name ‚Üí Mixed-effects models

Mixed-effects models

Fixed effects

  • Account for variation due to experimental factors
  • Represent average effects ‚Üí Population-level effects
    • Do not change or vary across groups or over time
  • Factors with limited, repeatable levels (e.g., group membership)
  • Basically the same condition effects ‚Üí Standard regression

Mixed-effects models

Random effects

  • Account for variation due to collecting data from a random sample
    of a much larger population
    • Account for variability within groups
  • Represent how much average effects (trends) vary across levels of
    some grouping factor (e.g., participants or items)
    • Particular groups might behave differently from the average
  • Should have at least 5 levels
    • More levels ‚Üí Preferable (Brown, 2021)
  • Intrinsically categorical (Winter, 2019)

Analyzing reaction times

  • Aim:
    • To test if sentence grammaticality affect reading times of L2 learners
    • Outcome variable: Reaction times (rt)
    • Two-level predictor variable: grammatical (GR) vs. ungrammatical (UG)

Concluding remarks

Mixed-effects modeling

  • Independence assumption ‚Üí Fundamental
  • Aggregation conceals data variability
  • State-of-the-art, flexible method:
    • Accounting for variability in linguisitic data
      • Simultaneously model participant and item effects
  • Consequently, our results are closer to the truth.
